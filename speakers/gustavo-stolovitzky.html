Talk Title

DREAM Challenges and Beyond: The Replicability Crisis and the Role of Community Benchmarking

Abstract

The explosive growth of data-driven modeling in biomedicine, fueled in no small measure by machine learning and AI, is starting to enable advances in reproductive health, rare diseases, cancer, and beyond. At the same time, biomedical research faces a profound replicability crisis: by some estimates, as many as three out of four published findings fail to replicate. In this lecture, I will present empirical evidence illustrating the depth and scope of this crisis.

Crowdsourced, collaborative benchmarking competitions—while not a panacea—provide a practical mechanism to counter the proliferation of non-replicable results, reduce the pitfalls of self-assessment, and raise standards of rigor at the community level. As a concrete example, I will discuss the DREAM (Dialogue for Reverse Engineering Assessment and Methods) Challenges and distill key lessons learned from two decades of organizing large-scale, open scientific competitions.

Finally, I will describe an emerging effort to build a consortium that bridges initiatives such as CASP, CACHE, DREAM, and others. While community-led benchmarking efforts have expanded rapidly, their proliferation has also fragmented research communities and limited collective impact. A coordinated coalition can reduce fragmentation, foster collaboration, establish shared validation standards, and build sustained trust in evolving AI methodologies, supporting their robust and reliable integration into biomedical research.
