Talk Title

Benchmarking to Infinity

Abstract

I will discuss FrontierMath, a mathematical problem-solving benchmark I developed last year, including its design philosophy, its limitations, and what we have learned from it about the current trajectory of AI systems.

I will then look much further out and speculate about what a “perfectly efficient” mathematical intelligence should be capable of. In doing so, I will argue that high-ceiling mathematical capability metrics—rather than near-term task performance—are essential for illuminating the path toward such an ideal.

This perspective reframes benchmarking not as a leaderboard exercise, but as a way to probe the fundamental limits of reasoning, abstraction, and formal understanding in artificial systems.

Bio

Dr. Elliot Glazer holds a PhD in Mathematics from Harvard University, where he studied under Hugh Woodin. His research focuses on set theory and formal systems, with particular interest in paradoxes related to the axiom of choice.

More recently, his work has explored the foundations of proof assistants and the design of mathematical benchmarks that stress-test reasoning at extreme levels of abstraction. He also enjoys developing mathematical puzzles in both finite and infinite settings.
